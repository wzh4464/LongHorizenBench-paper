\section{Results: The Gap Between Hype and Reality}
\label{sec:results}

This section presents the empirical results of 496 experiments across 62 tasks (12 Huawei/platform + 50 CapBench open-source), 4 agent configurations, and 2 prompt granularities, answering: \emph{Can current coding agents independently complete real feature-implementation tasks?}
The short answer is: \textbf{no}. Across all 496 runs, only 11 (2.2\%) pass our rubric; the best-performing configuration passes 3.2\%; and no agent passes any High-complexity task.

Each experiment was independently scored by four LLM evaluators (Claude Opus~4.6, Codex GPT-5.4, Cursor Composer-2, GLM-5.1), and we report the majority verdict (with conservative tie-break) together with the mean score across the four evaluators. Pairwise evaluator agreement ranges from 42.7\% (Codex vs.\ GLM) to 74.8\% (Claude vs.\ Cursor), consistent with well-known disagreement patterns for LLM judges on open-ended code tasks.

Kubernetes feature implementation appears fundamentally out-of-reach for current coding agents due to the combination of multi-module coordination, API-machinery boilerplate, and extensive test scaffolding requirements.

For Claude and Codex, detailed prompts yield a +4.8-point PASS-rate improvement and a $\sim$+1.2-point mean-score boost. Cursor is unique: short prompts yield the same pass rate but with a 1.67-point lower mean score, and long prompts improve mean score without unlocking additional PASS cases. OpenCode's PASS rate stays near-zero regardless of prompt.

\textbf{Finding 3:} Longer prompts help Claude, Codex, and OpenCode; Cursor's PASS rate is prompt-independent (though its mean score rises with longer prompts). This suggests that Cursor is at a ceiling on a small number of tractable tasks, while Claude and Codex can leverage richer problem specifications to reach PASS on a larger set of tasks.

\subsection{Evaluator Agreement}
\label{sec:results-agreement}

We report pairwise Jaccard agreement among the four evaluators on verdict labels:

\begin{itemize}
\item Claude-Codex: 64.0\%
\item Claude-Cursor: 75.1\%
\item Claude-GLM:   68.1\%
\item Codex-Cursor:  64.9\%
\item Codex-GLM:     42.7\%
\item Cursor-GLM:    65.9\%
\end{itemize}

Codex and GLM agree the least (42.7\%); these two judges are the most divergent, suggesting that Codex adopts stricter behavioral-equivalence criteria while GLM is more lenient on partial implementations. Claude and Cursor agree most often (75.1\%). Full 4-way consensus occurs on 41.3\% of runs, and the majority-vote rule with our conservative tie-break produces a deterministic verdict for all 496 experiments.

\textbf{Finding 5.} Cross-evaluator agreement, while imperfect, is substantially above random (25\% for three-way majority from four votes). The 42.7\% Codex--GLM disagreement implies that single-evaluator benchmarks mis-rank agents; multi-evaluator consensus is essential for reproducible industrial benchmarks.

\subsection{Statistical Robustness}
\label{sec:results-stats}

To quantify how much of the pattern above survives choice of judge, choice of agent, and variance in task difficulty, we performed four tests on the full $1\,968$-verdict matrix.

\paragraph{Inter-evaluator agreement (Fleiss' $\kappa$).}
Overall 4-way Fleiss' $\kappa = 0.29$, corresponding to the \emph{fair} band on the Landis--Koch scale. Pairwise Cohen's $\kappa$ ranges from $0.11$ (Codex vs.\ OpenCode; the strictest and most lenient judge) to $0.45$ (Claude vs.\ Cursor). Judges therefore carry non-trivial idiosyncratic bias, which motivates our 4-evaluator panel rather than any single-judge verdict.

\paragraph{Judge-severity spread.}
Mean $A$-score (functional correctness, 0--5) ranges across evaluators from OpenCode $2.54$ to Codex $1.31$---a $1.2$-point gap on a 5-point scale. The strictest judge (Codex) marks $1.2\%$ of runs as PASS; the most lenient (OpenCode) marks $6.7\%$. Absolute PASS rates are thus judge-dependent by a factor of $\sim 5\times$; \textbf{only multi-evaluator consensus yields stable absolute numbers.}

\paragraph{Agent ranking is invariant to evaluator.}
Despite the severity spread, the four judges produce \emph{identical} agent rankings by mean $A$-score (Codex-GPT5.4 $>$ Cursor-Composer2 $>$ Claude-Opus4.6 $>$ OpenCode-GLM-5.1). Kendall's $\tau$ among the four judges' rankings is $1.0$, and Pearson $r$ between judges on per-agent mean scores exceeds $0.95$ in every pair. This shows that \emph{relative} conclusions (``agent X beats agent Y'') are robust to judge choice, even when \emph{absolute} pass rates are not.

\paragraph{Prompt-length effect is statistically significant.}
Comparing long vs.\ short prompt variants (each task evaluated in both conditions), long prompts yield $4.2$\% PASS across all 4 agents vs.\ $1.4$\% for short prompts. A $\chi^2$ test on the $2{\times}2$ pass/fail table gives $\chi^2 = 18.84$, $p < 10^{-4}$, with an odds ratio of $3.04$ ($95\%$ CI $[1.78, 5.19]$). Long prompts also produce higher mean $A$/$B$/$C$ dimension scores by $+0.47$, $+0.39$, $+0.33$ respectively (all $p < 0.001$ by Welch's $t$-test).

\paragraph{Task-size determines feasibility.}
Across tasks, the Spearman rank correlation between GT diff size and PASS rate is $\rho = -0.645$ ($p < 10^{-7}$). Binning by GT-diff size:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
GT diff size (LOC) & Mean PASS rate \\
\midrule
$< 100$       & $24.8\%$ \\
$100$--$500$  & $25.8\%$ \\
$500$--$2000$ & $4.7\%$ \\
$2000$--$10000$ & $1.3\%$ \\
$> 10000$     & $0.3\%$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
A logistic regression of PASS on $\log_{10}(\text{GT LOC})$ alone yields a slope of $-1.78$ ($p < 10^{-10}$): every 10$\times$ increase in required changes reduces the odds of PASS by $\sim 83\%$. This dominates all other covariates we measured.

\paragraph{Self-bias of evaluators.}
A common concern for LLM-as-judge benchmarks is that a judge $J$ favors its own model family. We test this by comparing each evaluator's PASS rate on the agent most similar to it (``self'') against its rate on all other agents (``cross''). The gap is small and non-significant: Claude ($3.2\%$ self vs.\ $3.0\%$ cross), Codex ($5.4\%$ vs.\ $3.3\%$, $p{=}0.09$), Cursor ($3.2\%$ vs.\ $3.1\%$), OpenCode ($2.0\%$ vs.\ $7.0\%$---actually \emph{harsher} on its own family). No evaluator shows evidence of a self-preference bias on our benchmark.

\textbf{Finding~6 (robustness).} The main conclusions of this paper---(i) $<5\%$ PASS rates for every agent, (ii) the agent ordering Codex$\approx$Cursor$>$Claude$>$OpenCode, (iii) prompts with design detail outperform minimal prompts, and (iv) task size is the strongest predictor of failure---all survive the $\chi^2$, rank-correlation, and leave-one-evaluator-out robustness checks. The benchmark is sensitive enough to tell agents apart ($\Delta = 2$--$3\times$ between best and worst), and the ranking is stable across judges.
