\section{Results: The Gap Between Hype and Reality}
\label{sec:results}

This section presents the empirical results of 496 experiments across 62 tasks (12 Huawei industrial + 50 CapBench open-source), 4 agent configurations, and 2 prompt granularities, answering: \emph{Can current coding agents independently complete real feature-implementation tasks?}
The short answer is: \textbf{no}. Across all 496 runs, only 11 (2.2\%) pass our rubric; the best-performing configuration passes 3.2\%; and no agent passes any High-complexity task.

Each experiment was independently scored by four LLM evaluators (Claude Opus~4.6, Codex GPT-5.4, Cursor Composer-2, GLM-5.1), and we report the majority verdict (with conservative tie-break) together with the mean score across the four evaluators. Pairwise evaluator agreement ranges from 42.7\% (Codex vs.\ GLM) to 74.8\% (Claude vs.\ Cursor), consistent with well-known disagreement patterns for LLM judges on open-ended code tasks.

\subsection{Task-Family Effect}
\label{sec:results-family}

Our benchmark spans four task families: CANN operator kernels (C1--C5), MindSpeed distributed training (M1--M3), Kubernetes-family (K1--K4), and CapBench OSS (T01--T50). The PASS distribution differs dramatically across families:

Kubernetes tasks (K1--K4) are a categorical blind spot: \emph{zero} of 32 runs pass across all four agents and both prompt levels. CANN-family C++/AscendC tasks achieve the highest PASS rate (12.5\%), reflecting that Huawei-specific operator kernel code has relatively localised changes. CapBench OSS tasks average 1.25\% PASS, consistent with the overall 2.2\% but revealing that Kubernetes-class difficulty scales with project size.

\textbf{Finding 2 (Kubernetes Cliff).} All four agents fail on all Kubernetes-family tasks (0/32) regardless of prompt or agent capability---even the best-performing agent (Cursor, 3.2\% overall) scored zero on K1--K4. Kubernetes feature implementation appears fundamentally out-of-reach for current coding agents due to the combination of multi-module coordination, API-machinery boilerplate, and extensive test scaffolding requirements.

\subsection{Prompt Effect (Long vs Short)}
\label{sec:results-prompt}

Each of the 62 tasks was run with both a short ($\le 3$-sentence) and a long (detailed architectural spec) prompt. Table~\ref{tab:prompt} aggregates pass rates and mean scores by agent and prompt type.

\begin{table}[t]

\label{tab:prompt}
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lrrr@{}rrr}
\toprule
\textbf{Agent} & \textbf{Short PASS\%} & \textbf{Long PASS\%} & \textbf{$\Delta$PASS} & \textbf{Short Mean} & \textbf{Long Mean} & \textbf{$\Delta$Mean} \\
\midrule
Claude   & 0.0\% & 4.8\% & +4.8 & 5.08 & 6.28 & +1.20 \\
Codex    & 0.0\% & 4.8\% & +4.8 & 5.20 & 6.44 & +1.24 \\

OpenCode & 0.0\% & 1.6\% & +1.6 & 3.72 & 4.60 & +0.88 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

For Claude and Codex, detailed prompts yield a +4.8-point PASS-rate improvement and a $\sim$+1.2-point mean-score boost. Cursor is unique: short prompts yield the same pass rate but with a 1.67-point lower mean score, and long prompts improve mean score without unlocking additional PASS cases. OpenCode's PASS rate stays near-zero regardless of prompt.

\textbf{Finding 3:} Longer prompts help Claude, Codex, and OpenCode; Cursor's PASS rate is prompt-independent (though its mean score rises with longer prompts). This suggests that Cursor is at a ceiling on a small number of tractable tasks, while Claude and Codex can leverage richer problem specifications to reach PASS on a larger set of tasks.

\subsection{Evaluator Agreement}
\label{sec:results-agreement}

We report pairwise Jaccard agreement among the four evaluators on verdict labels:

\begin{itemize}
\item Claude-Codex: 64.0\%
\item Claude-Cursor: 75.1\%
\item Claude-GLM:   68.1\%
\item Codex-Cursor:  64.9\%
\item Codex-GLM:     42.7\%
\item Cursor-GLM:    65.9\%
\end{itemize}

Codex and GLM agree the least (42.7\%) — these two judges are the most divergent, suggesting that Codex adopts stricter behavioral-equivalence criteria while GLM is more lenient on partial implementations. Claude and Cursor agree most often (75.1\%). Full 4-way consensus occurs on 41.3\% of runs, and the majority-vote rule with our conservative tie-break produces a deterministic verdict for all 496 experiments.

\textbf{Finding 5.} Cross-evaluator agreement, while imperfect, is substantially above random (25\% for three-way majority from four votes). The 42.7\% Codex--GLM disagreement implies that single-evaluator benchmarks mis-rank agents; multi-evaluator consensus is essential for reproducible industrial benchmarks.
