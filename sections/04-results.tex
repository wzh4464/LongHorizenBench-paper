\section{Results: The Gap}
\label{sec:results}

This section presents the empirical results of 116 experiments across 12 tasks, 5 primary agent configurations, and 2 prompt granularities, answering \textbf{RQ1}: \emph{Can current coding agents independently complete enterprise-level feature implementation?} The short answer is no---the best configuration achieves 26.1\% PASS under our rubric---but the data reveals nuanced patterns across configurations, prompts, and task complexity.

Two configurations stand apart. Codex (gpt-5.4) achieves the highest pass rate (26.1\%, 6/23) with the highest mean composite score (9.83/15). A1 (Claude Sonnet) has pass rate (12.5\%, 3/24) but is dragged down by the K-series tasks, where it produced only planning documents and no code for K1, K2, and K3-long, resulting in 14 outright failures. \textbf{Finding 2:} \emph{The best configuration (Codex gpt-5.4) achieves 26.1\% PASS under our rubric---a meaningful step above prior agents, but far from production-ready.} Codex is the only configuration to pass medium-complexity tasks (C4, M2), demonstrating a qualitative capability gap over other configurations. Yet a 87.9\% non-pass rate on industrial tasks confirms that even frontier models require significant human oversight.

\subsection{Prompt Granularity Effect}
\label{sec:results-prompt}

Each task was run with both a \emph{long} prompt (detailed specification with design-level requirements and architectural guidance) and a \emph{short} prompt (one-line task summary). Table~\ref{tab:prompt-effect} shows the per-configuration delta.

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{Prompt granularity effect. Long = detailed specification, Short = one-line summary. $\Delta$Pass = difference in pass rate (percentage points). $\Delta$Comp.\ = difference in mean composite score.}
\label{tab:prompt-effect}
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lrrrrr}
\toprule
\textbf{Config} & \textbf{Long\%} & \textbf{Short\%} & $\Delta$\textbf{Pass} & \textbf{Long} & $\Delta$\textbf{Comp.} \\
\midrule
Codex (gpt-5.4)  & 33.3 & 18.2 & +15.2 & 10.42 & +1.23 \\
A1 (Sonnet)      &  8.3 & 16.7 &  $-$8.3 &  3.67 & $-$0.69 \\
A2 (Opus)        & 16.7 &  0.0 & +16.7 &  7.33 & +1.70 \\
A3 (Opus+Loops)  & 16.7 &  0.0 & +16.7 &  8.50 & +2.67 \\
MiniMax (M2.5)   &  9.1 &  0.0 &  +9.1 &  9.00 & +2.91 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Long prompts generally improve composite scores (+1.23 to +2.91), but the pass rate improvement varies dramatically.
Codex gains +15.2 percentage points~(pp); MiniMax's pass rate remains 0\% despite a +1.92 composite gain.
Detailed prompts help capable models cross the \textsc{pass} threshold while weaker models improve incrementally but remain below it.

\textbf{Finding 3:} \emph{Prompt granularity is the single strongest lever for improving agent performance.} Long prompts improve composite scores by +1.23 to +2.91 across most configurations, and improve pass rates by up to 16.7~pp for the strongest models. The effect amplifies existing model capability rather than substituting for it: strong models benefit most, while weaker models improve incrementally without crossing the pass threshold.

We note one counter-pattern: A1 shows a negative prompt delta ($-$8.3 pp), with short prompts outperforming long---its two short-prompt passes (C1-short, M2-short) involve tasks where concise specifications suffice, while its long-prompt pass (C1-long) is the only long-prompt success. This reversal does not generalize: all other configurations show non-negative prompt deltas.

\textbf{Finding 4:} \emph{Task complexity creates a steep capability cliff.} On low-complexity tasks, top configurations achieve 28--38\% pass. On high-complexity tasks, no configuration achieves any PASS verdict.

\subsection{Comparison with SWE-bench}
\label{sec:results-swebench}

SWE-bench~\cite{jimenez2024swebench} tasks are predominantly single-file bug fixes in Python repositories (1--3 files, $<$50 lines); our tasks range from 1 to 98 files and up to 11{,}107 lines, require new feature design, and span four languages.
Where frontier agents achieve $>$70\% on SWE-bench Verified, the best configuration in our benchmark achieves 26.1\% overall (33.3\% with long prompts); even on low-complexity tasks, our best pass rate is 37.5\%.

\textbf{Finding 5:} \emph{Under our benchmark, feature implementation appears substantially harder than bug fixing.} The gap grows with task complexity: on high-complexity tasks, even the best agent achieves 0\% pass on high-complexity tasks under our evaluation, suggesting that current benchmarks may underestimate the challenge of real-world coding agent deployment.
