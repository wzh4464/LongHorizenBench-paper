\section{Results: The Gap}
\label{sec:results}

This section presents the empirical results of 96 experiments across 12 industrial tasks, 4 agent configurations, and 2 prompt granularities, answering \textbf{RQ1}: \emph{Can current coding agents independently complete enterprise-level feature implementation?} The short answer is no---the best configurations achieve only 12.5\% PASS under our rubric---but the data reveals nuanced patterns across configurations, prompts, and task complexity.

Each experiment was independently scored by three LLM evaluators (Claude Opus~4.6, Codex GPT-5.4, and GLM-5.1); we report the \emph{majority verdict} (agreement of $\geq$2/3 evaluators) and averaged scores. The three evaluators agree unanimously on 62\% of verdicts and reach $\geq$2/3 consensus on 99\%; pairwise verdict agreement ranges from 69\% to 79\%, and per-dimension score spread averages $<$1 point, confirming robust cross-model calibration.

A 91.7\% non-pass rate across all configurations confirms that even frontier models require significant human oversight.

\subsection{Prompt Granularity Effect}
\label{sec:results-prompt}

Each task was run with both a \emph{long} prompt (detailed specification with design-level requirements and architectural guidance) and a \emph{short} prompt (one-line task summary). Table~\ref{tab:prompt-effect} shows the per-configuration delta.

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{Prompt granularity effect. Long = detailed specification, Short = one-line summary. $\Delta$Pass = difference in pass rate (percentage points). $\Delta$Comp.\ = difference in mean composite score.}
\label{tab:prompt-effect}
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lrrrrr}
\toprule
\textbf{Config} & \textbf{Long\%} & \textbf{Short\%} & $\Delta$\textbf{Pass} & \textbf{Long} & $\Delta$\textbf{Comp.} \\
\midrule
Claude (Opus 4.6)  & 25.0 &  0.0 & +25.0 &  7.8 & +2.6 \\
Codex (GPT-5.4)    & 25.0 &  0.0 & +25.0 &  8.4 & +2.2 \\
Cursor (Composer-2) &  0.0 & 16.7 & $-$16.7 &  5.7 & $-$1.3 \\
OpenCode (GLM-5.1) &  0.0 &  0.0 &   0.0 &  5.1 &  0.0 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Long prompts improve composite scores for the two strongest models (Claude +2.6, Codex +2.2) and amplify their pass rates substantially (+25.0~pp each). Both Claude and Codex pass 25.0\% of tasks with long prompts vs.\ 0\% with short prompts. However, the pattern is not universal: Cursor shows a \emph{negative} prompt delta ($-$16.7~pp), with both passes on short prompts. OpenCode shows no prompt effect, remaining at 0\% pass regardless of prompt detail.

\textbf{Finding 3:} \emph{Prompt granularity is the single strongest lever for improving performance of capable agents.} For the two strongest models, long prompts improve composite scores by +2.2 to +2.6 and pass rates by 25.0~pp. The effect amplifies existing model capability rather than substituting for it: strong models benefit most, while weaker models show no improvement or even negative effects.

We note one counter-pattern: Cursor shows a negative prompt delta, with short prompts outperforming long---its two passes (C1-short, C4-short) involve tasks where the model's autonomous exploration suffices, while long prompts may constrain its search strategy. This reversal does not generalize to the other configurations.

\textbf{Finding 4:} \emph{Task complexity creates an absolute capability cliff.} On low-complexity tasks, top configurations achieve 25\% pass. On high-complexity tasks, all four configurations achieve 0\%---no agent produces a production-equivalent implementation for tasks with 10+ ground-truth files.

\subsection{Comparison with SWE-bench}
\label{sec:results-swebench}

SWE-bench~\cite{jimenez2024swebench} tasks are predominantly single-file bug fixes in Python repositories (1--3 files, $<$50 lines); our tasks range from 1 to 98 files and up to 11{,}107 lines, require new feature design, and span four languages.
Where frontier agents achieve $>$70\% on SWE-bench Verified, the best configurations in our benchmark achieve 12.5\% overall (25.0\% with long prompts); even on low-complexity tasks, our best pass rate is 25.0\%.

\textbf{Finding 5:} \emph{Under our benchmark, feature implementation appears substantially harder than bug fixing.} The gap grows with task complexity: on high-complexity tasks, no agent achieves any pass (0\%), suggesting that current benchmarks may underestimate the challenge of real-world coding agent deployment.
