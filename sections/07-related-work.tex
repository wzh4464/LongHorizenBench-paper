\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Benchmarks for LLM coding agents.}
SWE-Bench and its variants~\cite{jimenez2024swebench,chowdhury2024swebenchverified,swebenchpro2025} popularized issue-resolution as the dominant evaluation: an agent must produce a patch making a failing test pass. Subsequent suites generalise this to feature-style work: FeatureBench~\cite{multiswebench}, CapBench, and SWE-Lancer-style task collections measure agents on multi-file changes. Closer to our setting, Agentless~\cite{agentless} and harness-engineering proposals~\cite{harness2025,confucius} report that lightweight per-task scaffolds (file enumeration, test-derived oracle, scoped retrieval) close most of the gap to fully agentic systems on these benchmarks. Our contribution differs in two ways: (i) we evaluate \emph{commercial harnesses as deployed}, not research scaffolds, and (ii) the workload is real Huawei industrial PRs plus 50 OSS replicas, so the result is a deployment-readiness statement rather than a leaderboard.

\textbf{Industrial reports on agent rollouts.} A small but growing literature documents real production rollouts of LLM-based developer tools: Passerine~\cite{mathai2024passerine} (Google's bug-fix bot), AutoBug~\cite{autobug} (Microsoft), and the LogSage incident-pipeline study~\cite{logsage}. These reports converge on a similar message---narrow tasks land, wide-scope agentic ones do not. Our work extends that picture to feature-PR-grade work, replaces single-judge scoring with a calibrated four-LLM panel, and surfaces a sanitization protocol against in-repo ground-truth leakage that to our knowledge has not been documented before.
