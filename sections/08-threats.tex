\section{Threats to Validity}\label{sec:threats}

\textbf{Single-run stochasticity.} Each task--agent--prompt cell was run once; aggregate patterns (Kubernetes 0/32, long-prompt PASS lift, harness-tier ordering) are stable across all 62 tasks, but individual cell verdicts may shift on re-run. The deployment-relevant signal (PASS rate of $\sim$1--2\%) sits well below typical re-run variance for these agents on bug-fix benchmarks.

\textbf{Product-level confound.} Each agent pairs a specific commercial model with a specific harness; we cannot disentangle the two. We therefore phrase observations such as ``harness leverage is substantial'' as observations on \emph{deployed products}, not as causal claims about harness-vs-model contribution. The 92.7\% PASS-vs-non-PASS agreement between our judge panel and three blinded senior reviewers indicates that the verdicts themselves are not a panel artefact.

\textbf{Strict production-equivalence rubric.} Our rubric counts a run as PASS only if it implements the reference behaviour and updates the same handwritten files (within the structural-equivalence rules in \S\ref{sec:study-design}). A valid alternative implementation that diverges from the upstream PR will be marked non-PASS even if a human reviewer would accept it. The 1.6\% headline therefore lower-bounds merge-readiness rather than measures it directly. A spot check of 30 PARTIAL cells (uniformly sampled) found roughly 4 cases where a senior reviewer would have accepted the agent's alternative; we therefore treat $\sim$3\% as a soft upper bound on agent-feasible merge rate under our task corpus.

\textbf{Calibration scope.} Human-rater calibration was conducted only on the 96 Huawei-internal runs. The 400 CapBench OSS runs are scored by the LLM panel alone, on the assumption that panel-vs-human agreement transfers across task families. A blinded re-rate of a sample of CapBench runs is left as future work.

\textbf{Single-run, single-prompt sampling.} Every cell of the 4 agents $\times$ 62 tasks $\times$ 2 prompts grid was run once. Stochastic decoding could move borderline runs across the PASS threshold; the headline 1.6\% is therefore an estimate, not a tight bound. We mitigate by reporting outcome distributions, not just point estimates, and by treating sub-percent differences between agents as below the noise floor.
