\section{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:threats}

\textbf{Construct validity.}
Every experiment is independently scored by three LLM evaluators from different model families (Claude Opus~4.6, Codex GPT-5.4, and GLM-5.1), and we use the majority verdict ($\geq$2/3 agreement) as the final outcome.
This cross-validation design mitigates single-evaluator bias, but shared blind spots across model families may persist.
Unanimous agreement is 62\%, and $\geq$2/3 consensus reaches 99\%; pairwise agreement ranges from 69\% to 79\%, with per-dimension score spreads averaging $<$1~point.
Our rubric measures production-level equivalence to the merged implementation rather than purely functional correctness; it may miss dimensions such as maintainability or security.

\textbf{Internal validity.}
Each task--configuration--prompt cell was executed once; stochastic variation means individual results may not represent expected performance, though aggregate patterns across 96 experiments provide robustness.
All cross-configuration comparisons are \emph{product-level} assessments confounding model and framework differences (\S\ref{sec:configs}).
During initial K4 experiments, Codex exploited insufficiently sanitized git history via \texttt{git cherry-pick}; we remediated by re-sanitizing all repositories and re-running affected experiments, confirming no ground-truth extraction in post-sanitization trajectories.

\textbf{External validity.}
Our 12 tasks span four languages and three complexity tiers but may not generalize to other enterprise contexts (e.g., Java, Rust, fully proprietary codebases).
Agent capabilities evolve rapidly; our early-2026 model snapshots will be superseded, though we expect relative patterns (prompt effect, complexity cliff) to persist.

\textbf{VCS history as information channel.}
Codex proactively searched git history and web documentation, while other agents varied in their use of these information channels.
Neither behavior constitutes ground-truth leakage (sanitization removed target commits), but the information asymmetry means performance differences partially reflect tool-use strategy rather than pure model capability.
