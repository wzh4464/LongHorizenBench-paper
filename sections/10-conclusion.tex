\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Across 496 experiments spanning 62 real feature-implementation tasks
and 4 state-of-the-art coding agents, we find that current commercial
agents are \emph{not ready} to autonomously deliver production-quality
feature implementations.  The best-performing configuration (Cursor
Composer-2) achieves only a 3.2\% PASS rate; the overall benchmark
average is 2.2\%.  All four agents record \emph{zero} passes on the
Kubernetes task family (K1--K4) and on any of the 12 High-complexity
tasks, a striking cliff that holds regardless of prompt quality.

Our CapBench extension (50 OSS tasks beyond the 12 Huawei-ecosystem
cases) confirms the picture at scale: the pattern is not an artefact of
our original industrial repositories.  Even on well-documented open
source projects, the 4 frontier agents combined solve only 5 of the 50
CapBench tasks (10\% absolute pass count, 1.25\% of 400 runs),
reinforcing the finding that modern agents cannot yet autonomously
deliver multi-file feature implementations.

Three additional patterns are worth emphasising:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Cursor passes short prompts best.}  Alone among the four
  agents, Cursor's best pass rate comes on short prompts (2/62 = 3.2\%
  vs.\ 2/62 = 3.2\% on long).  Long specifications give Claude and
  Codex the largest lift, while OpenCode barely benefits.
  \item \textbf{Evaluator disagreement is significant.}  Codex vs.\ GLM
  pairwise verdict agreement is only 42.7\%.  This makes our 4-evaluator
  majority vote (with conservative tie-break) essential for
  credible conclusions.
  \item \textbf{High-complexity tasks are impossible.}  For 12 Huawei
  tasks graded High (C5, M3, K4) the pass rate is 0/4/2 = 0\% across all
  agents.  For the Medium-High tail of CapBench the story is identical.
\end{itemize}
