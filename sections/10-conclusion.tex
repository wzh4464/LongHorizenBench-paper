\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper revisits coding-agent evaluation from the perspective of industrial, system-level feature implementation. Across 496 runs on 62 tasks, current commercial agents produce very few merge-ready PRs under a strict-equivalence rubric: the 50-task main corpus yields 4/400 SE-PASS outcomes, all on one trivially scoped single-file task, and 0/392 on the remaining non-trivial tasks. The calibration subset reinforces the task-shape conclusion: subsystem-contained operator/framework tasks occasionally pass, while Kubernetes-class platform tasks do not.

The study also identifies what helps and what remains unsolved. Long engineering-proposal prompts improve draft quality, but do not make hard feature tasks merge-ready. Deterministic HW/GT coverage reports ground completeness scoring, but multi-evaluator judging is still necessary because single LLM judges disagree substantially, and human calibration is needed to anchor the panel. Failure analysis shows that semantic errors dominate, while coverage gaps and borderline partials remain practical targets for better scope and test validation.

Future work should re-run key cells to estimate variance, extend human re-rating to a stratified main-corpus sample, and evaluate agent designs that directly target cross-module reasoning, test generation, and invariant preservation. Until those improvements appear in measured SE-PASS rates, coding agents are best viewed as subsystem-contained task drafters rather than autonomous owners of industrial feature delivery.
