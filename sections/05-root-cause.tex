\section{Root Cause Analysis}
\label{sec:root-cause}

Of 96 experiments, 88 result in non-PASS verdicts (48 PARTIAL, 39 FAIL, 1 no majority).
This section analyzes the failure modes to determine whether the gap reflects fundamental model-capability limitations or engineering-addressable shortcomings.

%% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Failure Mode Taxonomy}
\label{sec:failure-taxonomy}

We classify the 88 non-PASS experiments into four failure modes based on the averaged evaluation scores across three independent evaluators:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Incomplete coverage} ($B < 2$): The agent touched too few files or components to constitute a meaningful implementation. 43 cases (48.9\%).
  \item \textbf{Partial coverage} ($2 \leq B < 4$, $A \geq 2$): The agent covered some required files but missed key components, producing a recognizable but incomplete implementation. 36 cases (40.9\%).
  \item \textbf{No output} ($A < 1$ and $B < 1$): The agent produced no meaningful code---crashed, timed out, or generated no changes. 8 cases (9.1\%).
  \item \textbf{Structural mismatch} ($C < 2$ with $A, B \geq 2$): The agent covered the right scope with working code but used a fundamentally different architectural approach. 1 case (1.1\%).
\end{itemize}

The dominant failure mode is incomplete coverage (48.9\%), not semantic misunderstanding (1.1\%).
Combined with partial coverage, 89.8\% of non-PASS outcomes stem from the agent failing to cover the full scope of required changes.

\finding{Completeness (dimension B) is the dominant bottleneck: it is the lowest-scoring dimension in 69\% of non-PASS experiments, with mean $B = 1.71/5$ vs.\ mean $A = 2.21/5$. Only 1.1\% of failures reflect structural mismatch; the remainder stem from insufficient coverage scope.}

%% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Engineering-Addressable vs.\ Model-Capability Gaps}
\label{sec:eng-vs-model}

To separate engineering problems from model-capability problems, we compare dimensions A (Functional Correctness) and B (Completeness) for each non-PASS experiment.
When $B \leq A$, completeness is the binding constraint: the agent understands the problem but fails to implement the full scope.
When $A < B$, the agent covers the right scope but implements incorrectly, indicating a model-capability limitation.

Of 88 non-PASS experiments: 74 (84.1\%) have $B \leq A$ (engineering-addressable), 6 (6.8\%) have $A < B$ (model-capability limited), and 8 (9.1\%) produced no output.

\finding{84.1\% of non-PASS outcomes are engineering-addressable: the agent's functional understanding (A) exceeds its implementation completeness (B), suggesting that better scope control---not better model reasoning---would close the majority of the gap.}

\subsection{Complexity-Driven Degradation}
\label{sec:complexity-degradation}

Table~\ref{tab:complexity-rootcause} shows how completeness degrades with task complexity.

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{Completeness degradation by complexity tier among non-PASS experiments. B$<$2 = critically incomplete.}
\label{tab:complexity-rootcause}
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
\toprule
\textbf{Tier} & \textbf{Non-PASS} & \textbf{Mean B} & \textbf{B$<$2} & \textbf{B$<$2 \%} \\
\midrule
Low     & 27 & 2.28 & 10 & 37.0 \\
Medium  & 37 & 1.67 & 20 & 54.1 \\
High    & 24 & 1.15 & 21 & 87.5 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

On high-complexity tasks, 87.5\% of non-PASS experiments are critically incomplete ($B < 2$), compared to 37.0\% on low-complexity tasks.
Mean B drops from 2.28 (low) to 1.15 (high), confirming that agents struggle with scope management as cross-component dependencies increase.
Three examples illustrate the pattern:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{K4} (58 HW files, Go): The best agent (Claude) covers 31/58 files (53\%); the weakest (Cursor) covers 1/58 (2\%). Even partial implementations miss authorization hooks, generated client code, and e2e test scaffolding.
  \item \textbf{C5} (25 HW files, C++/AscendC): Coverage ranges from 0/25 (OpenCode) to 12/25 (Codex, 48\%). No agent reaches PASS.
  \item \textbf{M3} (9 HW files, Python): All agents cover $\leq$1/9 files. No agent achieves PASS on any prompt variant.
\end{itemize}

\finding{Task complexity creates a completeness cliff: on high-complexity tasks, 87.5\% of non-PASS experiments are critically incomplete ($B < 2$), and mean $B$ drops from 2.28 to 1.15. Agents fail at scope discovery, not at per-file implementation quality.}

All four agents exhibit the same failure pattern: B consistently trails A, confirming that incomplete coverage is the universal bottleneck.
Codex achieves the highest non-PASS mean B (1.95), reflecting broader file exploration. With both Claude and Codex tied at 12.5\% PASS, Codex's higher mean composite score (7.36 vs.\ 6.50) suggests that exploration breadth yields better partial implementations even when it does not cross the PASS threshold.

File coverage data corroborates this analysis.
On K4, Claude covers 31/58 HW files while Cursor covers only 1/58---a 30$\times$ difference that directly explains their respective PARTIAL vs.\ FAIL verdicts.
On C2, all four agents cover 4/4 HW files yet all receive PARTIAL, indicating that when coverage is not the bottleneck, functional correctness (A) becomes the binding constraint.
