\section{Harness Engineering}
\label{sec:harness}

The previous sections established that no agent configuration reliably completes industrial feature implementation (Section~\ref{sec:results}) and that failures cluster around engineering-addressable gaps (Section~\ref{sec:root-cause}).
This section examines whether engineering interventions---execution-time harnesses and detailed specifications---can close the performance gap.

%% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Definition and Taxonomy}
\label{sec:harness-def}

We define \textbf{Harness Engineering} as the practice of augmenting a coding agent's execution environment with structured inputs, validation loops, or process constraints---without modifying the underlying model.
We distinguish three intervention points:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Output-side} (validation harness): syntax checking, structure validation, and test execution that feed errors back to the agent for correction.  Our A3 configuration adds the Loops harness to the A2 baseline.
  \item \textbf{Input-side} (specification harness): detailed task descriptions, file-level scope constraints, or architecture guidelines provided before generation begins.  Our long prompts serve this role.
  \item \textbf{Process-side} (pipeline harness): enforced phases such as discovery-before-implementation, mandatory test generation, or CI-gated iteration.  No configuration in our study implements this level.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Aggregate effect.}
A3 wins 12 of 23 comparisons, ties 3, and loses 8.
The mean composite improvement is +0.52 points (A2: 6.52, A3: 7.04).
A3's PASS rate (8.3\%) is comparable to A2's (8.7\%), with PARTIAL+ rates of 79.2\% and 73.9\% respectively.

\textbf{Domain dependence.}
The harness effect is strongly domain-dependent.
On CANN/torch\_npu tasks (C-series), A3 wins 8 of 10 comparisons with mean delta +2.90.
On MindSpeed tasks (M-series), A3 wins 3 of 6 with mean delta +0.67.
On Kubernetes tasks (K-series), A3 wins 2 of 7 with mean delta $-$1.14.
The Loops harness validates syntax and file-structure conventions specific to C++/AscendC codebases; these checks are less applicable to Go's generated code patterns.

\finding{The Loops harness wins 12/23 head-to-head comparisons with mean composite improvement of +0.52/15. Its benefit concentrates on convention-heavy C++ codebases (CANN: +2.90 mean $\Delta$) and reverses on Go codebases (K8s: $-$1.14 mean $\Delta$).}

\subsection{Input-Side Harness: The Prompt Effect}
\label{sec:harness-prompt}

Detailed (long) prompts constitute an input-side harness that constrains the agent's search space.
Table~\ref{tab:prompt-effect} (in \S\ref{sec:results-prompt}) shows the effect across configurations.

\finding{Detailed specifications improve PASS rates by up to 16.7 percentage points and composite scores by +1.23 to +2.91/15 across most configurations.
The effect is strongest for models already near the PASS threshold.}

\subsection{Harness Limitations and Future Directions}
\label{sec:harness-limits}

Even with both harness types active, the best configuration (Codex + long prompts) reaches only 33.3\% PASS.
The remaining gap stems from:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Test generation:} No harness enforces test writing; on K4, Codex wrote 9 test files while Claude~A3 wrote 0.
  \item \textbf{Architecture reasoning:} Validation can catch syntax and structure errors but cannot verify that cross-module wiring is semantically correct.
  \item \textbf{Discovery depth:} The harness does not control how thoroughly agents explore the codebase before writing code.
\end{itemize}

We identify three levels of harness improvement, ordered by estimated difficulty:
(1)~\emph{language-aware validation} (adapt checks to Go/Python/C++ conventions; estimated +2--5\% PASS);
(2)~\emph{fix-cycle loops} (feed validation errors back for agent correction; estimated +10--15\% PASS); and
(3)~\emph{architecture-aware planning} (enforce discovery-before-implementation phases; estimated +5--10\% PASS).

\finding{The output-side Loops harness improves composite scores by +0.52 points (mean) and wins 12/23 head-to-head comparisons, but its effect is domain-dependent: strong on C/M-series tasks (+1.60 mean), negative or neutral on K-series ($-$0.43 mean). Input-side prompt engineering (long vs.\ short) yields up to +16.7pp pass rate improvement. The combination suggests that harness design must be language- and domain-aware to be effective.}
