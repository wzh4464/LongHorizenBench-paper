Multiple platform teams at Huawei face a concrete deployment decision in 2026: whether to route portions of their feature backlog---Kubernetes controllers, CANN/\texttt{torch\_npu} accelerator operators, MindSpeed training features---through commercial coding agents.

This paper's primary contribution is a \emph{four-gate deployment policy} adopted in Huawei's Ascend NPU rollout: (i)~a task-class gate restricting agent invocation to bounded operator/refactor work, (ii)~a mandatory long-form specification template, (iii)~a pre-review CI gate enforcing declared file scope and test coverage, and (iv)~a 3-of-4 LLM-judge acceptance panel. Cross-cutting platform work---Kubernetes-class controllers, protocol-level refactors---remains under human authorship until the gates close on a measurable failure mode.

Each gate is motivated by evidence from a leakage-safe industrial audit. We evaluate four commercial agents on a two-tier benchmark: a 50-task \emph{main evaluation corpus} of long-horizon feature tasks drawn from 15 open-source projects (CapBench; median 57 files changed, 6{,}905 LOC diff) and a 12-task \emph{calibration subset} of Huawei industrial tasks used for human-rater pilot calibration (92.7\% SE-PASS-vs-non-SE-PASS agreement; panel strictly stricter). Across 496 runs (62 tasks $\times$ 4 agents $\times$ 2 prompts), the main corpus yields 1.0\% SE-PASS; excluding one trivially scoped single-file outlier (T10), the rate is 0/392 = 0.0\%. The calibration subset confirms rubric discriminant validity: bounded operator tasks reach 7.5\% SE-PASS while Kubernetes-class platform work returns 0/32.

Under our protocol, current agents are not ready for autonomous feature delivery on these task families---but the routing pipeline makes them useful as \emph{gated drafters} for bounded tasks.
